# Trademark and naming policy

The **code** of this federation is free and forkable under AGPL-3.0-or-later.
The **names** are not the code. This policy protects the names so that a fork
cannot rename-and-claim the legitimacy that the original's key continuity,
provenance history, and live relay carry. The moat is legitimacy, not source —
and a name is how legitimacy is recognized.

> **Status: common-law policy, not a registration.** No marks are registered
> yet. This file asserts the intent and the usage rules now; a formal
> registration can follow if and when there is revenue to support it (a decision
> reserved to the architect — it is external spend). Until then these are
> good-faith community norms, not legal claims of a registered mark.

## The names

`trinity`, `myc`, `mycelium` (as used here), `omega` / `OMEGA-64`, `liquid`, the
`Φ` / `OMEGA1` protocol marks, and any future conformance mark such as
**"Substrate Court verified."**

## What you MAY do (nominative fair use)

- State, truthfully, that your work **uses**, **is compatible with**,
  **extends**, or **interoperates with** trinity / myc / omega / liquid.
- Fork the code and run it — that is the whole point of AGPL. Say "based on
  trinity" or "a fork of myc."
- Use the names in documentation, articles, talks, and research to refer to this
  project.

## What you MAY NOT do

- Use the names, or confusingly similar ones, as **your own project or product
  name** in a way that implies this is the official/original federation.
- Imply **endorsement, affiliation, or certification** by the federation that
  does not exist.
- Apply a conformance mark (e.g. **"Substrate Court verified"**) to a deployment
  that has **not** passed the actual court verification — the mark means the
  verification happened, and monetizes verification, not enclosure.
- Rename a fork to a near-identical name to harvest the original's reputation,
  key continuity, or provenance. The code is yours to fork; the _identity_ is
  not silently transferable.

## Why this exists

A well-funded actor cannot take AGPL code closed (the §13 network-copyleft
blocks that), but it _could_ fork, rename, and market itself as the "real" one.
What stops that is not the licence — it is that legitimacy here is anchored in
things a fork cannot silently copy: the key registry and its quorum-gated
amendment chain, the signed provenance ledger, the live relay continuity, and
public receipts. A name is the handle on all of that. Protecting the name
protects the commons from capture, and keeps any future **"Substrate Court
verified"** conformance mark meaningful — the one revenue path that monetizes
_verification_ rather than enclosure.
